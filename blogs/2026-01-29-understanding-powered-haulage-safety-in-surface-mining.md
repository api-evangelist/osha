What Jobs Can I Get With an OSHA Card? Jessica Barrera Thu, 05/21/2026 - 08:00 An OSHA card can open doors to a wide range of safety-conscious careers, from entry-level roles on construction sites to supervisory positions in manufacturing, healthcare, and beyond. Completing an OSHA Outreach Training course shows that you understand essential workplace safety practices and know how to prevent hazards before they happen. In this guide, we’ll highlight the top industries hiring OSHA-trained workers and the types of jobs your card can help you qualify for. OSHA 10 vs OSHA 30: What Is the Difference? Workers who finish OSHA training are issued an OSHA card (also known as a DOL card ) by the U.S. Department of Labor (DOL) . The card serves as proof that you have completed training on workplace risks and safety. However, it’s important to choose the OSHA card that aligns with your long-term goals, and for that, you first need to understand the difference between an OSHA 10 and an OSHA 30 card. Choosing between OSHA 10 and OSHA 30 depends on your career goals and the level of responsibility you want on the job. Both courses teach essential safety standards, but the right one helps you get ahead faster in your field. Here’s a comparison to help you decide: Course Who It’s For Time Commitment Typical Job Level Common Industries OSHA 10-Hour New workers who want to build safety awareness and stand out in entry-level applications About 10 hours Entry-level positions Construction, manufacturing, warehousing, hospitality, healthcare OSHA 30-Hour Team leads, supervisors, and safety managers ready to take on more responsibility and lead by example About 30 hours Supervisory or management roles Construction, utilities, oil and gas, manufacturing, environmental services What Industries Recognize OSHA Training? OSHA training is recognized across many high-risk industries because it helps workers prevent accidents, keep projects on schedule, and protect employers from costly fines and downtime. Some of the main industries that recognize OSHA training are: Construction : OSHA training shows you understand jobsite hazards, from working at heights to operating equipment and handling materials. Manufacturing: Helps you work safely around machinery, production lines, and chemical processes while following lockout/tagout procedures. Healthcare: Supports safe handling of patients, equipment, and infectious materials, including bloodborne pathogen awareness. Transportation & Logistics: Reduces accidents in warehouses, loading docks, and yards by improving material handling safety. Oil & Gas: Prepares workers for hazards like flammable materials, confined spaces, and high-pressure systems. Agriculture: Helps reduce injuries from heavy equipment, livestock, and environmental hazards. General Industry : Applies broadly across warehousing, utilities, hospitality, and retail. Employer requirements vary by state laws, company policies, and specific projects, so it’s always important to confirm what’s required for your target job. What Jobs Can I Get With OSHA 10? OSHA 10 is ideal if you’re starting out or transitioning into a hands-on role. It shows employers you can follow safety rules, recognize hazards, and contribute to a safe work environment. Because OSHA 10 is widely recognized, listing it on your resume can help you qualify for more jobs and get interviews faster. Roles that commonly require or benefit from OSHA 10 include: Construction laborer: Assists with site prep, cleanup, and basic tasks while following safe practices. General construction worker: Supports residential, commercial, or civil projects. Skilled trades assistant: Works alongside electricians, plumbers, or HVAC technicians. Landscaper: Uses equipment like mowers and trimmers safely in outdoor environments. Maintenance technician: Performs repairs while working safely around equipment and confined spaces. Warehouse associate: Handles materials and inventory in fast-paced environments. Manufacturing line worker: Operates equipment and avoids common production hazards. What Jobs Can I Get With OSHA 30? OSHA 30 prepares you for leadership roles where you oversee teams and enforce safety practices. It demonstrates a deeper understanding of workplace safety and compliance. Roles that commonly require or benefit from OSHA 30 include: Site supervisor: Oversees daily operations and ensures safety compliance. Facility manager: Manages building operations, vendors, and maintenance teams. Safety coordinator: Supports safety programs and conducts training. Safety inspector: Identifies hazards and ensures regulatory compliance. Manufacturing supervisor: Leads teams and reduces workplace incidents. Foreman or lead worker: Directs teams and models safe work practices. Do Employers Value OSHA Cards? Yes—many employers value OSHA cards because they demonstrate safety knowledge and reduce onboarding time. An OSHA card: Shows commitment to safety: Demonstrates you take workplace safety seriously. Reduces training time: Covers foundational safety knowledge upfront. Helps meet job requirements: Many projects require OSHA-trained workers.
